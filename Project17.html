Bambook Shelf

Marking Out

The first (and most important step!) for accurate joinery is accurate marking lines. So, with a sharp knife I set about marking out where to cut (and not cut, also a very important distinction to make!).

Clearing Out the Mortises

I used my handy coping saw to clear out most the waste in the mortises which saved a lot of time!

Tidying Up the Mortises

With a pair of nice sharp chisels, I could start tidying up the mortises, taking it all the way back to the marking line. A great advantage of a marking line is that it leaves a small groove to locate the chisel into.

Cutting Out the Tenons

I recently purchased a Japanese saw (interchangeable cross- and rip-cut blades) and it cuts great! Nice accurate markings on the tenons really help with cutting close to the lines then chiselling back to the line for a good crisp edge

Test Fitting

This image really shows the payoff for accurate marking out! There’s hardly any visible gap around the tenon! Now I had done one joint, it was time to apply the same level of detail to the other 3.

Assembly Fitting

With all the joints (finally!) cut, I assembled the whole shelf to check I had not accidently cut the joints to make the bookshelf was skewed. Thankfully, it was very tidy. The joints were tight enough to hold together but easy enough to assemble by hand.

Finishing Up

The last process of this project was to apply finish to the parts. I liked the look of an oil finish, but I was worried about the oils potentially staining the books in the long run, so I went with a wax finish. The wax finish gave it a really nice warm colour and a very shiny polish (the result of a lot of elbow grease!).

Final design

Here’s the final design! I really like how this turned out, it would look great in a creative office or even a book-worms’ bedroom! The through-mortise-and-tenon really accentuates the skill involved in the joinery as well as giving a strong structure! I chamfered the ends of the shelves to avoid pointy ends and to give a bit more visual interest up-close.

Skills

I had a great time practising marking out and working to the marked-out lines, which will definitely be a skill I will use again. Bamboo is a really good material to work with as it is very strong but cuts very well. I can’t wait to try other joinery techniques now!
