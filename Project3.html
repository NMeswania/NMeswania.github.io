Educational Robot Scheme: Electroics Hardware

Project Brief

The Educational Robot Scheme was a semester long University project in a group of 6. We were tasked with creating a workshop for students to educate them on coding using the Dyson 360EYE robot. Our solution workshop included 5 challenges for the students to solve using a modified 360EYE: it could have either a gripper-arm or a ping-pong ball launcher. With these attachments, the students would create a script in Google Blockly that would control the robot to solve the challenge.

Other related sub-projects: Communication, UI design

Sub-system Brief

The additional circuitry that would control the gripper and launcher attachments was to fit inside the 360EYE waste chamber as we would not be using the suction of the vacuum cleaner (I know that’s the main feature of a vacuum cleaner, but it imposed far too many sizing restrictions), plus it was the only space for any new electronics.

Challenges

There were strict size restrictions - all of the new hardware had to fit inside the waste chamber. That included a microcontroller, power regulation circuitry, wireless communication boards, sensors and motors.

Why New Hardware?

You may be wondering why there needed to be any new attachments and circuitry in the first place. The gripper and launcher attachments allowed students to interact with the environment around them, something they couldn’t do previously. As for new circuitry, Dyson could not supply us with detailed circuit diagrams and e-CAD of the robot for IP protection reasons. With these restriction in place, we decided to create our own control system independent of the robot.

Part Selection

The microcontroller was chosen on size basis - an Arduino Nano. Fortunately, the Nano could sufficiently meet the pin-requirements. With the project budget set by the University, the cheaper components were selected. During testing, I found that despite their low cost, the performance was more than satisfactory for all components.

Power Regulation

To allow for more attachments to the robot that may require 12V of power, I designed a simple power regulation circuit seen in this image.

Automatic Attachment Recognition

To aid with the “plug and play” feel of the robot, I decided to add a feature to the robot that would automatically detect which attachment was connected. This would also prevent students from sending instructions for the wrong attachment which could lead to explosions. It uses a voltage divider connected to an analog-pin with each attachment using a unique resistor.

Final Design

The render seen here is the final circuit. It included a Bluetooth module (not pictured), power regulation circuitry for the gripper/ launcher attachments, 12V LED MOSFETS (for some visual feedback on the robot) and automatic attachment recognition logic.

Further Work

The next step is integrating the electronics I had been working on with the 360EYE electronics, though this cannot be done without access to the innards of the robot.

Skills

The stripboard circuit design was very challenging to compact the circuit into a small space, it would have been far worse without the use of Fritzing. I also improved my circuit design, e-CAD, component sourcing, cost estimation and prototyping.
